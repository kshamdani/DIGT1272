Legal & Technical Standards

The Rules That
Govern Accessible
Design

Three key frameworks define the legal and ethical obligations of web developers in Canada and globally, from international guidelines to federal and provincial law.

Three Key Frameworks

Governing Frameworks

01
W3C International

WCAG 2.1: Web Content Accessibility Guidelines

Published by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C), WCAG 2.1 is the globally recognized technical standard for web accessibility. It defines three levels of conformance across 13 guidelines built on the four POUR principles.

Most legal mandates worldwide reference WCAG 2.1 Level AA as the minimum compliance target. Level AA covers 50 success criteria addressing the most significant barriers for people with disabilities.

Level A: The minimum is the failure to meet these criteria makes content inaccessible to some users entirely.

Level AA: The standard legal requirement addresses the widest and most critical barriers.

Level AAA: The highest level is recommended for content primarily serving people with disabilities.

02
Canadian Federal Law

Accessible Canada Act (ACA) - 2019

Canada's Accessible Canada Act came into force in 2019 with the goal of creating a barrier-free Canada by 2040. It mandates that federally regulated entities (including banks, broadcasters, and federal government) identify, remove, and prevent barriers across all areas including ICT systems.

Organizations must publish accessibility plans, track progress, and respond to feedback. Compliance is enforced by the Accessibility Commissioner, with administrative monetary penalties for non-compliance.

Scope: Federal government, Crown corporations, federally regulated industries (transport, banking, telecom).

ICT requirements: Digital services, websites, and software must be accessible to employees and the public.

Reporting: Annual progress reports and accessibility plans must be published and open to feedback.

03
Ontario Provincial Law

AODA / Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation

Ontario's Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) was passed in 2005, establishing a framework for developing mandatory accessibility standards. The Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR) directly governs websites and web content for public and private organizations in Ontario.

Organizations with 50+ employees were required to meet WCAG 2.0 Level AA by January 2021. The Information and Communications Standard under IASR covers websites, web content, and web-based applications.

Public sector: Required WCAG 2.0 AA compliance for all web content by 2016.

Private sector (50+ employees): Full WCAG 2.0 AA required by 2021.

Enforcement: Directors of the Accessibility Directorate of Ontario conduct compliance audits.

WCAG Conformance

Understanding Conformance Levels

WCAG success criteria are organized into three conformance levels. Meeting higher levels means reducing more barriers for more users.

A
Level A: Minimum
The baseline. Failure to meet these criteria makes content inaccessible or unusable for some users. Covers 30 success criteria including keyboard access and alt text.
30 Success Criteria
AA
Level AA: Standard
The legal and industry standard. Addresses the most significant barriers. Required by the ACA, AODA, EU Web Accessibility Directive, and Section 508 in the US. Covers 50 total criteria.
50 Success Criteria
AAA
Level AAA: Enhanced
The highest level. Not required for entire sites (some criteria cannot be universally applied), but recommended for content specifically targeting people with disabilities. 78 total criteria.
78 Success Criteria
Developer Responsibility

How Liable Should Developers Be?

One of the central questions of this project: how much legal and ethical responsibility should developers bear for ensuring their digital services are accessible to people with disabilities?

Legal frameworks like the ACA and AODA say organizations are liable, but most enforcement focuses on large institutions rather than individual developers. The ethical question runs deeper: when you build something inaccessible, who is responsible for the exclusion that follows?

"Accessibility is not a feature — it is a social trend." The longer accessible design is treated as optional, the more people are excluded from digital civic life.

Arguments in the Debate

For Stronger Liability

  • Exclusion causes real, measurable harm to users
  • Legal accountability drives adoption of accessible practices
  • Infrastructure analogy: buildings must be accessible, so must websites
  • Standards like WCAG make compliance achievable and testable
  • Retrofitting costs more than designing accessibly from the start

Considerations & Tensions

  • Small developers may lack resources without support or guidance
  • Edge cases in accessibility can be genuinely complex to solve
  • Liability without education risks punishing ignorance over intent
  • Organizational vs. individual liability needs clear legal distinction
  • Enforcement must be proportionate and context-sensitive
